    What is TNRHumane?

    TNRHumane is an Android mobile application that allows users to report homeless cats and alert volunteers or animal organizations to come and Trap, Neuter, and Return feral cats to its environment. The app will help find homes for abandoned animals, provide food/medical care, connect caretakers through social media feature, and act as a humane solution to reduce feral cats overpopulation.

    OVERVIEW

    Homeless cats multiply quickly in the wild. A pair of unfixed cats can multiply up to 32 cats within 16 months. While many often feed them, others see them as a nuisance. Thus, creating problems such as:

    • Animosity towards homeless cats and feeders
    • Risk of human-transmitted diseases
    • Risk of animal abuse
    • Kittens born suffer and die in the wild.

    In order to ensure animal welfare, the community is facing the following problems:

    • Problem: Unable to keep track of the homeless cat population.
    • Solution: Allow users to report homeless cats sighting to TNR for population control.
    • Problem: Unable to promote TNR program effectively.
    • Solution: Keep users in-touch with the community.
    • Problem: Unable to promote TNR program effectively.
    • Solution: Keep users updated with the community.
    • Problem: Unable to keep track of homeless cat health records.
    • Solution: Built-in database to keep track of homeless cats health records.

    Here are the results that shaped the foundation of TNRHumane:

    • 53%

      chose to do nothing

      decided to take actions when facing a stray

    • 56%

      chose to feed and care for the animal when turned away

    • None

      have heard of stray animals app

    • Survey Takeaways
    • TNR program is still very foreign to many
    • More are willing to help homeless cats so my app design will gear towards animal advocates

    COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

    Pawpular is a social media platform for pet owners to share photos of their pets such as dogs, cats, reptiles, birds, aquatic animals, mammals, etc. The platform UI is very similar to Facebook and Instagram. Pawpular has its own growing community in the dogs and cats categories, but none in other categories. Moreover, its content needs more organizations by eliminating unpopular features and services that have not been out yet.

    StrayGlobe is a one-of-its-kind mobile app that let user report stray animals. The app was developed in India and their map works worldwide. It has no support for interactivity among users. Because the app works worldwide, the chances for someone in the U.S to use it to report a stray animal and actually have someone who knows about StrayGlobe to come help is extremely low. Thus, it is a very ineffective application.

    ASPCA is one of the few animal welfare organizations that developed a mobile app. ASPCA’s goal is to inform pet owners how to prepare for disasters. They also encourage users to take actions by donating or sign petitions for animal rights. Other services include storing pet’s health record and social feed where they post updates about their events, announcements, and status.

    Based on the survey and the competitive analysis, I was able to ideate the basic structure of the app.

    PHASE 3: VISUAL DESIGN

    WIREFRAMES: TURNING IDEAS INTO DESIGNS

    I created wireframes to connect the concepts, user flows, and branding together on paper, then digitized them using Figma.

    USABILITY TESTS

    I then used these wireframes to performed usability tests using Maze. There were 16 participants.

    Users were asked to perform the followings:

    Task 1: Go through the tutorial and report a stray

    Task 2: Create an account

    Task 3: Set up a recurring payment

    Task 4: Turn off auto payment

    Task 5: View nearby stray cats reports

    Task 6: Add a report to a to-do list

    Task 7: Update report status

    I found that:
    • Task one question was confusing. It asked users to perform 2 tasks in one, this cause confusion and only 54% on participant successfully completed the task
    • Improve language used for new users to better understand the mission
    • Add a feature for volunteers to locate each other

    LOGO SKETCHES

    A left ear being tipped in a homeless cat means it has been trapped, neutered/spayed, vaccinated, and returned to its environment. I wanted to show this symbol in the logo. I started out with some sketches. Halfway through the process, I decided to use a picture of a stray cat that I’ve helped, this way, I could realistically portray his face shape. The logo then paired with a catchphrase and typeface.

    VIEW SKETCHES

    BRANDING IDENTITY

    Since the platform promotes TNR program, which works with homeless cats, I wanted to use a cat logo that represents their skittish, shy, unapproachable characteristics. Furthermore, the tone and environment of the platform have to be fun, this way, it will encourage users to take actions. The application will be using outdoor, so the UI will have to be accessible under the sunlight, yet easy to see at night when volunteers take actions.

    COLOR & STYLE GUIDE

    Colorwise, I went with the classic hue that represents compassion. Red and pink were my top choices. I went with pink because using red as primary color could be problematic. For example, red usually associated with an error.


    As for typeface, I picked Prompt. It is user-friendly and easy to see. Moreover, Google Application recently updated their typefaces. I noticed that it is rounder and has more latter space. Because TNRHumane is an Android application, using a similar typeface provide familiarity.

    PHASE 4: TESTINGS

    USER PREFERENCE TESTS

    I picked 3 elements that potentially need improvement then created 2 versions for each element and conduct a preference test using Usability Hub.

    • Locating donating receipts

    • 65% picked option B

      I decided to test this element because while conducting usability test, participant hesitated when locating payment information. I wanted to see how they would like their receipts located.

    • Posting method

    • 68% participants picked option B

      I decided to pick option B, however, while double checking with the Material Guidelines, it was recommended to use a floating button with caution when navigation options are present. Since my navigation tab included words, I thought it would be crowded with the floating button. Moreover, users are more likely to keep in touch with events and TNR deals than writing a post. Thus, I gear the screen’s focus toward posts from organizations than for a user to write posts

    • Image vs Illustration

    • 61% picked option B

      The responses between these alternatives were very competitive. The results proved to be statistically significant. Option B may be performing better due to random chance alone. I decided to went with option B. I feel that users can relate more to the picture rather than Illustration. Especially when it comes to animal welfare. In real scenarios, I would like to use real pictures of kittens who had been saved by the community who used the app.

    2ND ITERATION

    The 2nd iteration entails improvements from the feedback I received from the preference tests.

    USABILITY TEST ROUND 2

    This iteration then used to perform another usability tests: 1 recorded remote test and a Maze test (14 - 21 participants)


    Overall, participants had the most problem with the 1st task, causing many to give up on the rest of the test. Task 1 asked participants to fill out a report without sign in. Many did the opposite and clicked the “sign in” button, which caused confusion and participants were unable to complete the task. Others clicked “skip”, which allow them to complete the task, but they did not know what the report is for, what is TNR, or why they are filling out a report.


    I learned that users are impulsive and will click on options that are present to them. Ultimately, I decided to get rid of both “skip” and “sign in” button to guide the users through the tutorial.

    FINISHING TOUCHES: A USER PERSPECTIVE

    WHAT I LEARNED

    Brainstorming in multiple sessions: The hardest part of this project was how to get started. The concept is fairly new and there were limited apps with this concept. I knew some user frustrations and goals from personal experience as a volunteer, I have some ideas and features I wanted to create but was overwhelmed when thinking about organizing the user flow. Ultimately, I did many brainstorming sessions, taking breaks in between to refresh my mind and repeat. This process helps me put my thoughts out in intervals, be able to analyze ideas, and branch out from those ideas.


    Sorting Competitors when there is none: Another challenge was finding a competitor to analyze. There was only one app developed in India that addresses the stray animals problems. None in the U.S. So I decided to pick my competitors based on the basic features I wanted to develop. For example, I wanted to have a social feature to connect with local volunteers and allow users to keep updates from fundraising events, training, low-cost clinics, etc. I studied FaceBook, Instagram, Pawpular, ASPCA, Twitter, LinkedIn. This helped me learn basic social media structures, what they all have in commons, why are they different? What are their differences? How their users interact with the platform?


    More options, more confusions: While conducting the 2nd usability test, I see that users will click on buttons to explore or find the shortest way to complete the task. This caused confusion as they did not know what they are doing, its purpose, and how they got to the screen they are at.
