    What is TNRHumane?

    TNRHumane is a mobile application that allows users to report homeless cat sighting and alert volunteers or animal organizations to come and Trap, Neuter, and Return feral cats to its environment. The app will also help find homes for abandoned animals, provide food/medical care, and act as a humane solution to reduce feral cats overpopulation. Besides helping homeless cats, TNRHumane acts as a social platform for local caretakers and animal advocates to connect.

    COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

    Pawpular is a social media platform for pet owners to share photos of their pets such as dogs, cats, reptiles, birds, aquatic animals, mammals, etc. The platform UI is very similar to Facebook and Instagram. Pawpular has its own growing community in the dogs and cats categories, but none in other categories. Moreover, its content needs more organizations by eliminating unpopular features and services that have not been out yet.

    StrayGlobe is a one-of-its-kind mobile app that let user report stray animals. The app was developed in India and their map works worldwide. It has no support for interactivity between users and volunteers. Because the app works worldwide, the chances for someone in the U.S to use it to report a stray animal and actually have someone who knows about StrayGlobe to come help is extremely low. Thus, it is a very ineffective application.

    ASPCA is one of the few animal welfare organizations that developed a mobile app. ASPCA’s goal is to inform pet owners how to prepare for disasters. They also encourage users to take actions by donating or sign petitions for animal rights. Other services include storing pet’s health record and social feed where they post updates about their events, announcements, and status.

    What can I do with this information?

    Google Drive, Dropbox, and Box all known for a specific feature. So I thought, “What should virtBox be known for?”. After careful considerations of virtBox’s target audience and goals to help online learners, I decided it should be known for its organization.

    • Google Drive: accessibility
    • Dropbox: synchronization
    • box: collaboration
    • virtBox: organization

    Sounds about right.

    PHASE 3: VISUAL DESIGN

    WIREFRAMES: TURNING IDEAS INTO DESIGNS

    I created paper wireframes to connect the concepts and user flows, then digitized them using Balsamiq. The differentiator of virtBox is its organization feature, which I found an opportunity to highlight in the dashboard navigation pane. This section helps the user easily locate their files based on the file type.

    USABILITY TESTS

    The wireframes were then used to performed usability tests: 1 in-person, and 2 remotes. This helped me see how users interact with the sketches without visual clues.

    Users were asked to perform the followings:
    • Signing up for an account
    • Adding a piece of content
    • Organizing a piece of content
    I found that:
    • Buttons need to be more obvious
    • There was confusion whether or not a piece of content was successfully added
    • Users could not exit popup windows
    • No differentiation of file types
    • A user commented she would use virtBox because she could easily find her files with the dashboard navigation pane!
    Takeaways

    The dashboard navigation pane layout is heading in the right direction.

    BRANDING IDENTITY

    Since the product is created for online learners, I want to establish a bright, clean, and motivative environment. The name virtBox is the combination of academic growth and the concept of online storage. Virt is short for virtual, representing the virtual workspace for users. Virt in French represents the color green and green represent growth. When users use virtBox for their online learning experience, they are growing academically. Box represents a platform to store all files in one place. virtBox’s logo combines the letter”B” sideways inside a laptop. The internal “B” is a derivative of earlier sketches of a virtual reality glasses.

    COLOR & STYLE GUIDE

    During my research, I realized all three of my competitors used blue as their primary color. Further research shows blue is the most widely liked color. I decided to use blue, but also wanted to differentiate my product from the competitors, thus, indigo was chosen. Indigo was paired with a blue-gray color at different opacity to create variety. All combinations passed the WCAG AA and WCAG AAA test with accessibility for those with color blindness.

    PHASE 4: TESTINGS

    USER PREFERENCE TESTS

    I picked 3 elements that potentially need improvement then created 2 versions for each element and conduct a preference test using Usability Hub.

    • Laid out navigation menu vs collapsed navigation menu

    • 72% picked option B

      Since they were unable to view what entails under “categories”, they had concerns of having to scroll down if the “categories” option is expanded. However, there were only 3 options under “categories”, which users will not have to worry about this concern. Thus, I decided to implement option A for the next iteration.

    • One button to add files vs 2 buttons to add files

    • 59% of users picked option B

      This test was the hardest. Both options started off very even. Some stated they like the simplicity while others think it is less confusing to have 2 buttons. I decided to move on with option A to keep the interface clean and to following the rule of progressive disclosure. If given more time, I would definitely perform more tests. This is one of those situations were what designers think is best might not be what the users want.

    • Icons vs non-icons

    • 100% users picked option B

      Users stated the icons help them scan quickly through the navigation. I originally skipped the icons to keep things simple and completely overlooked the important of icons. I decided to use option B to move forward.

    USABILITY TEST ROUND 2

    The 3rd iteration then used to perform another usability tests: 2 in-person and 1 remote. Overall, participants did not have trouble sorting content and identifying file types. Major improvements need to be fixed are with Invision. Many screens were not displaying correctly. Otherwise, participants liked virtBox features; especially the color-coded feature. They were also excited to have a semi-final product that they can experience compared to the clickable wireframes.

    Things to improve:
    • Popup windows need to pop up correctly. Need to blur out the background.
    • Consistent content format.
    • “CREATE NEW FILE” options need to link to the correct destinations.

    FINISHING TOUCHES: A USER PERSPECTIVE

    This iteration utilized the semi-final product with improvements made based on the feedback from the usability tests. Most edits were made by adding colors to sections to make information stand out and easy to read. Others were spell check and alignment check.

    WHAT I LEARNED

    This project was challenging for me. I enjoyed the visual design process, however, I had a hard time differentiating my product from the competitors. The current market is saturated and I did not want to create another replication of what’s already on the market. Competing with a strong competitor like Google Drive was tough. However, I learned to create the product specifically for a targeted audience based on the user survey results. This significantly helped me plan out my project.


    Another challenge I encountered was not being specific enough when creating the user survey questions. When analyzing the result, I realized asking vague questions led to vague answers. Because not all participants leave their contact information at the end, it was hard for follow-up and ask them to provide further explanation.


    If given more time, I would do a preference test and usability test after every iteration. I would also perform the tests with more random participants instead of distributing them to mostly the design community. I feel that this caused the results to be skewed.
